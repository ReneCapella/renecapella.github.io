From the Assignment

1. Modify the mesh of two STLs to produce a single printable STL. You may use whatever STLs you want, e.g. downloaded from Thingiverse or elsewhere! When exporting the STL from Rhino, it should pass all checks for rapid prototyping.

2. Please make (a start on) a lamp! Using your lamp innereds' measurements as a point of departure, make a lamp that can be assembled around the innereds. You must make the lamp using digital fabrication techniques from prior weeks. You should be able to remove the lamp from the innereds again, so you cannot attach the lamp to the innereds with glue, fasteners, adhesive, etc. This will require you to think carefully about the interface between your parts. Imagine your lamp being in a domestic setting---perhaps it will not be wildly shaken, but it should not fall apart with normal use. For the rest of the lamp, get creative!

Part 1: Getting Meshy

  1. Find two meshes you like or want to work with
  2. Import both to Rhino
  3. MeshRepair both meshes to assess, fix issues, and create a "good mesh"
  4. Remove/Slice/BooleanDifference/etc the pieces that need removed to ensure clean merging
  5. Commence merging
  6. Check your new unified mesh- Is it "good"? Do you need more repairs?

Part 2: Lamps!

  1. Find your light innered.
  2. Measure it! Capture its dimensions!
  3. Reflect on the digital fabrication techniques you've explore thus far
  4. Determine a good location that needs a lamp
  5. Imagine a lamp that compliments this space's function and the desired mood you want to experience in this space. Do you want to laugh? Be creative? Get stuff done?
  6. Sketch it/capture it/start prototyping

Process Documentation

Merging Meshes!

Meshes are easy to mess up. By merging two meshes together, you're able to manifest these issues. For instance: intersecting faces, holes, crashing software, and more!

Process

Finding meshes should have been an easy task, but without know what I wanted to blend, I struggled finding objects without perusing thingiverse.

Originally, I found models at Flyingarchitecture.com. These were great for rendering, but exporting to STL was much harded because meshes were not closed. My MeshBooleanUnion hung at 83% for a long while before crashing Rhino.

It wasn't all bad, though, because it provided space for more practice with rotation and placement of model parts.

After crashing twice, I found the snail and the bearded viking. No regrets; he's adorable.

Alas, BeardSnail had too many intersecting faces. 71 at one point. Reducing the mesh got this down to 40, but I couldn't do much better.

I printed him anyway.

Then, I found the angry cat, who, after lots of tinkering, not only looked great printed, but also was rated a "good mesh":)

Lamp

I am creating a lamp for my family's book nook. We are a family of 5 in 1000sqft, so every space matters. This space is where many of go to read, tinker, and play games.

We are also a floor-seated household (we like furniture low to the ground). Finding lamps to accommodate this is difficult. We current have a lamp that blinds us from below. We also have lights that shine from above, but it creates unwanted shadows.

We also have a fireplace here. During the winter we might use this once or twice -- but it's a barren black hole otherwise.

So I'm going to see if I can brighten this gloom hole up.

After brainstorming shapes and getting all the basic ideas out of my head, I noticed a pattern of layers and translucency.

So, I ran with this for a small while.

The layered shapes caught, and I thought of sandwiching them between clear acrylic -- and considered how to place this in the fireplace so that colorful shapes were seen from front and shaped-casted color (there's a difference!) was casted behind in the cavern.

I like this the most. I think it's delicate and complex. I think it's graceful and not too full of itself. It's friendly.

How else could I layer these color panes? Hanging them? Getting the materials felt pressing, so I ordered these while I sketched,and installed better exhaust for the laser printer (not pictured).

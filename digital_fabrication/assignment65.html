Assignment 6.5

From the Assignment

Design and fabricate a 2-part (silicone) mold. You will need to use the mold to cast at least 4 identical parts (e.g. in plaster). Design your master part in CAD first, then design your mold, then design a mold to cast your mold in. So meta! You need to 3d print your molds-for-molds. Then you need to cast your molds! Then you need to cast your parts!

Finishing the Molding and Casting!

  1. Obtain the new Oomoo 30
  2. Read the MATERIAL SHEET: Put on PPE! Gloves! Eyewear!
  3. Follow directions. Equal parts A and B
  4. Stir thoroughly
  5. Pour silicon into the 3D printed molds
  6. Let set for 4 hours!
  7. Remove your new, floppy mold!
  8. Mate the two halves, mix the hydrostone, and pour it in
  9. Wait twenty minutes: peel out your cool new cast

Process Documentation

This is where we left off last week:

This all unfolded over Saturday afternoon.

I was anticipating the silicone mixtures A/B to be cured in the bottle again... so I had two backups. I know have a lot of silicone, because the first one worked!

Lots of anxiety about where to pour this in the mold to avoid bubbles.

Getting this out was a problem: I designed the mold casing with a bottom. Removing the silicone required I used a firm, flat edge to pry it out - potentially damaging the casing or cast.

It worked out okay, though.

The hydrostone mixed well - I found it to be very forgiving. The amount we were given for the project was perfect.

Because I didn't use runners, when I poured in the mixture, I needed the two mold halves to "breathe" a bit. I needed a little to drain through the cracks so I could fill it completely (I know, the point of runners).

I felt more secure using the vice to hold it in place.

So adorable. Peeling these out each time was a complete joy.

Later in the evening, I went through and filed several of them. I left a few with the 3D print traces and one with the spout still attached. Having the different versions tells a small story.
